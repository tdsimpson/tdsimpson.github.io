01 — Context & problem

Breaking into Europe shouldn't require building a distribution company.

Craft wine and spirit brands outside Europe face a fragmented, expensive path in: importers, warehouses, compliance, local sales partners, and e-commerce — each its own relationship, contract, and learning curve. Most brands either stay home or burn time and money stitching it together themselves.

Lexir's bet was a single platform that handled that stack — so a brand could focus on the product and the market, not the plumbing. My job, over three years, was to make that platform real, legible, and worth switching to.

Tension 01

Old model, new expectations

Traditional distribution is relationship-heavy and opaque. Brands wanted direct consumer connection without giving up local expertise.

Tension 02

Complexity vs. speed

Every new market added legal, logistics, and partner layers. The product had to absorb complexity so clients didn't have to.

Tension 03

Trust before features

Prospects weren't asking for more buttons — they needed to believe Lexir could actually replace how they'd always sold in Europe.

02 — My role

Owning the product end to end.

I led the brand dashboard and e-commerce platform, owned the roadmap and go-to-market strategy, and translated requirements for a remote design and dev team of five across Europe and South America. That meant weekly prioritization, client-informed specs, release planning, and being the person who connected what we were building to why it mattered commercially.

I wasn't the only person at Lexir — but on product, I was the throughline: from interview insight → roadmap → shipped feature → how we explained it on the homepage.

03 — Product & platform

Two surfaces, one business.

Brands lived in two places: a dashboard to manage their European presence — inventory, orders, analytics, market settings — and a consumer storefront where their customers actually bought. The product work was making both feel like one coherent system, not two tools bolted together.

Roadmap priorities that came up again and again: data visualizations brands could act on, subscription and replenishment models, and configurability per market without rebuilding the stack each time.

04 — What clients needed

Interviews surfaced the real blockers.

I ran regular client and prospect interviews — not as a one-off research phase, but as an ongoing input to the roadmap. Three themes kept coming back:

They couldn't see the whole picture. Dashboards and reporting had to answer "how am I doing in this market?" without exporting to a spreadsheet.

They didn't understand the model. Many prospects thought Lexir was another importer. Product and marketing had to explain the shift together — what we replaced, what we didn't.

They needed proof before expansion. Brands wanted to win in one market before betting on six. The platform had to support that crawl-then-run motion.

"A lot of our growth came from making the value legible, not just shipping features."

05 — Key decision

Growing faster by building smarter.

Early on, we were building nearly everything custom — expensive, and slow to change. I made the case to move the right pieces onto no-code and low-code tools instead of hand-building them, which cut implementation costs by 60% and freed the team to focus on what actually differentiated Lexir.

That efficiency wasn't a side win — it was part of how we scaled from one market to six and grew the client base 8× over three years. The decision was as much about where we spent engineering time as about tooling.

06 — Go-to-market

How we told the story.

The platform was the heart of the business, but not the whole of the job. I shaped Lexir's messaging and ran marketing across video, email, social, ads, SEO, events, and community — including the explainer on the homepage. In an industry built on traditional distribution, explaining the shift was part of the product.

I wrote the script, recorded the voiceover, and produced the music and audio; animation was handled by a partner. The same positioning showed up across every channel — the throughline was always: make the value obvious before the sales call.

07 — What it taught me

Three years of product in a market that didn't want a new model.

Lesson 01

Explaining the shift is part of the product

In an industry built on traditional distribution, the hardest work wasn't features — it was explaining why the old way breaks down and why switching is worth it.

Lesson 02

A smaller, working product beats a big, broken one

You can't build the perfect platform in a vacuum. You need enough working product to prove people want it — ship, validate, then invest in the full vision.

Lesson 03

PM is connecting insight to commercial outcome

Client interviews, roadmap calls, GTM copy, and cost decisions weren't separate workstreams — they were the same job: make Lexir easier to buy, use, and expand with.
