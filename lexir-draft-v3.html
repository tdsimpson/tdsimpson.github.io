01 — The need

Why Lexir had to exist

Before Lexir, a craft brand entering a new European market really only had one option, a traditional distributor. They buy stock cheap, control where it lands and how it’s priced, and only want to deal in bulk. Brands end up with slim margins, little attention, and no real way to test a market at a small scale. Lexir works the other way around. Brands keep ownership of their stock, pricing, and margin, and Lexir runs the infrastructure underneath it all.

Traditional Lexir
Control & margins Buys stock cheap, sets the terms, keeps most of the margin Brand keeps stock, sets prices, holds onto much higher margins
Cost & speed Long middleman chain; onboarding can take months One platform for import, warehouse, shop, and delivery — built to move faster
Focus & data Easy to get lost in a big portfolio; brands get little performance data Brand owns its presence — dashboard shows what’s working, backed by local sales partners on the ground
Starting & scaling Often wants bulk; new distributor for every market Start small (e.g. ~100 bottles), then add markets on the same rails

Built mainly for craft brand founders entering Europe, with buyers mostly premium cocktail bars and restaurants.

02 — My role

Owning the product end to end

I led the development and release of the brand dashboard and e-commerce platform, owned the product roadmap, and helped shape go-to-market (positioning, channels, and how we explained Lexir) while writing specs for a remote design and engineering team of five across Europe and South America.

I ran regular client interviews to find feature gaps, usage patterns, and where prospects got stuck on the value proposition. That shaped both the roadmap and how we explained Lexir: what it actually did for a brand, and why switching was worth it. Making that value obvious mattered to our growth as much as shipping features.

03 — The product

Dashboard for brands, storefront for buyers

Brands managed products, markets, orders, and analytics in the dashboard. Buyers purchased through the storefront. For craft products, story and photography mattered a lot, so I put a lot of early energy into the catalog: copy, photos, and layout.

One tool I’m proud of is the margin calculator (Figma with our designer). A brand could enter bottles, price, market, and alcohol percentage, and see a per-bottle breakdown of salesperson cut, Lexir’s cut, tax, and shipping. That math was specific to us, so it stayed custom-built.

04 — What we learned

Saying yes to the model wasn’t enough

Even after brands liked the idea, they still asked distributor-era questions. The most common: “What prices will you set on my products?” We’d say: you set them. That kept happening. It showed that handing someone new tools wasn’t enough. We also had to help them use those tools in a real market: pricing, labeling, taxes, and finding people on the ground.

We couldn’t personally answer every question for every brand in every city. One response was building toward local sales specialists as in-market guides, plus written guidance and the margin calculator so brands could see the economics before they committed.

05 — A key decision

Stop rebuilding checkout and shop basics

Early on we were custom-building a lot of the shop, including checkout. That’s a solved problem. We could pay for a mature platform, or have a small team spend weeks recreating a weaker version. The same was true for large parts of the storefront and onboarding.

I pushed us onto a no-code / low-code platform so we could move faster. Founders were hesitant at first. We’d already started down the custom path, and switching felt like throwing that away. I showed we could reach the same place in weeks instead of months, and free engineering for Lexir-specific work like the margin calculator.

Moved off custom

  • Much of the shop platform
  • Checkout-style flows
  • A lot of onboarding / landing

Stayed custom

  • Margin calculator
  • Other Lexir-specific logic
  • Custom embeds when we hit tool limits
06 — How we explained Lexir

We had to teach the new model

Most people only knew traditional distribution, so marketing had to show the old way and the new way. I worked on landing pages, catalog copy, social, and the homepage explainer (script, voiceover, music/audio; a partner did animation).

The idea I wanted someone to leave with: Lexir is how craft brands break into new markets while keeping control of stock and pricing, keeping higher margins, and facing a lower barrier than a traditional distributor.

On outreach, Instagram DMs outperformed cold email. A lot of people treat email as a chore; a DM from a brand they already find interesting is more likely to feel welcome. I ran the account and iterated messaging with sales based on what got replies.

07 — What I took away

What this work taught me

01

Explaining a new model is part of the product

Features aren’t enough if people still think like distributors. Pitch, guidance, and tools have to move together.

02

Don’t rebuild commodity software when you’re small

Buy or configure the basics. Spend engineering on what only Lexir needed.

03

Tools aren’t the same as adoption

Guidance and local partners mattered as much as the dashboard.

04

I would have focused the public story more

Lead harder on B2B “rails for brands,” and separate it more cleanly from B2C.

What I want someone to take away: I’m comfortable owning messy, end-to-end work at a startup. I care a lot about whether people understand what they’re looking at, and I try to balance that with what the business needs to ship and sell.
